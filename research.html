TCAD Simulation
- Process simulation

We simulate actual semiconductor fabrication steps—such as ion implantation, diffusion, etching, and deposition—to generate accurate doping profiles and device structures. Key structural features, including pillar ripple formation and junction depth, are determined at this stage.

- Device simulation

Based on the fabricated structure, we apply external bias conditions and solve the Poisson equation together with the carrier continuity equations. This enables a physics-based analysis of I–V characteristics, electric-field distribution, and impact ionization behavior. Beyond observing simple trends, our focus is on calibrating physical models—including mobility degradation, Shockley–Read–Hall recombination, and other advanced mechanisms—to ensure close agreement with real device behavior.

- Mixed mode simulation

Single-device characteristics alone cannot explain the transient behavior observed during high-speed switching.

This approach enables detailed analysis of gate ringing caused by the interaction between parasitic gate inductance, parasitic source inductance, and the device's input capacitance. It also allows extraction of dynamic gate-to-drain capacitance and dynamic drain-to-source capacitance under realistic high-dV/dt switching conditions—phenomena that conventional LCR meters cannot observe due to depletion-region modulation. Furthermore, by simultaneously monitoring circuit-level waveforms and internal carrier distributions, this method provides deep insight into failure mechanisms such as shoot-through events and avalanche behavior that occur during inductive switching.
Characterization
- Static Characterization

Many studies rely solely on measuring I–V curves to check breakdown voltage and on-state resistance. Our work goes further by uncovering the physical origins that fundamentally determine device efficiency. We analyze not only the intrinsic material properties of silicon and wide-bandgap semiconductors such as SiC and GaN, but also structural effects including parasitic JFET behavior and depletion-width modulation. Through this limit analysis, we identify the true lower bound of the specific on-state resistance — the true material limit that governs ultimate device performance. In addition, our research demonstrates that simply shrinking the cell pitch does not guarantee improved performance. For superjunction devices, we determine the optimal balance between charge compensation and pillar width, enabling designs that maximize the figure of merit and push the theoretical boundaries of device efficiency.

- Dynamic Characterization

In real power-electronics systems, device failure almost always occurs during switching transients. Our studies reveal that manufacturer-provided small-signal capacitance curves do not represent actual behavior under high-dV/dt and high-di/dt switching conditions. Using our mixed-mode simulation framework and a custom five-terminal measurement method, we separate and extract dynamic gate-to-drain capacitance and dynamic drain-to-source capacitance as they vary during real switching events — information fundamentally inaccessible to conventional LCR meters. Furthermore, we show that gate ringing and voltage overshoot are not caused solely by parasitic inductances in the circuit. These phenomena also arise.
